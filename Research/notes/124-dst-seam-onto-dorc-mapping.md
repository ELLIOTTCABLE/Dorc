## Findings

- f24. **The decisive structural fact: Dorc's leaf-execution seam is PROCESS-LEVEL; DST's seam is IN-PROCESS — and that gap is the whole crux.** `plans/077` is explicit: the seam wraps/spawns *arbitrary external programs* (docker, apt, a vendor installer) and remote hosts; it is "process-level, not in-process (not a library/FFI API)." DST (FDB/madsim/sled), by contrast, *requires* the system-under-test's own logic to run **inside** the simulator process with every IO replaced by an in-process fake [A-fdb-sigmod-paper-2021]. You cannot run `apt`, a vendor installer, or a remote `sshd` "inside the simulator." So DST and `plans/077` are seams at *different layers* — but they meet at one decision point. +SURE.
- f25. **What DST *can* test for Dorc vs what it can only *mock*.** CAN: the **orchestration kernel under network chaos** — the multi-host schedule (`kSCHEDULE` DAG walk), partial-failure handling, retries, the plan/apply state machine, the three-valued verdict (`notes/112` f50), "host 7 of 12 drops right after the mutation lands but before the ack." That is precisely Dorc's *differentiating* correctness vs Ansible. CANNOT: whether the opaque external program actually worked, or the real remote host's behaviour (the user's own example — reconfigure sshd, does the tunnel renegotiate/restore state) — the **mocked edge**, whose fidelity is bounded by your understanding of it (Eaton consideration-3), and which FDB explicitly could not test: "unable to test third-party libraries or dependencies… we have largely avoided taking dependencies on external systems" [A-fdb-sigmod-paper-2021]. +SURE.
- f26. **(adversarial — the real fork for the human) DST's payoff for Dorc ∝ how much genuine concurrency/ordering/failure-interleaving complexity lives in the orchestrator vs at the mocked edge.** For a database the kernel *is* the system, so DST tests almost everything. For Dorc a large share of "did it actually work" correctness lives at the opaque-external-program / remote-host edge that DST can only mock — and that edge is exactly the `axis-quadrant` non-containerizable region (f9) and the *oracle/probe/idempotency* job (parked). So **DST is necessary-not-sufficient for Dorc**, and its value rests on the orchestration layer being non-trivial — which `kAGENTLESS` single-controller multi-host coordination under partial failure does make it. ~SUSPECT; this is the fork to weigh, not a settled claim. The clean division it implies: **DST for the orchestration kernel; oracles+probes+idempotency for the edge.**
- f27. **The chokepoint is dual/triple-use — and that makes the DST seam nearly free to reserve.** `dorc_exec(host, leaf)` is simultaneously: (prod) `plans/077`'s wrap-and-spawn of the real program; (trace) the seccomp/fsatrace/`DORC_LEAF_ID` attribution point; and (test) the **DST substitution point** where a simulator returns a *synthetic outcome* (ok / timeout / drop-after-mutate / partition / reorder) instead of spawning anything. `plans/077` already requires this one indirection for tracing; reserving it cleanly *also* reserves the DST seam. +SURE this is the cheap, retrofit-hostile thing to bank day-1 (it is the asymmetry from notes/122 §6).
- f28. **Rust realization, mapped to Dorc's process-level reality (from notes/123).** madsim's tokio-shim is the *least* directly applicable — Dorc's leaf IO is process-spawn + ssh, not tokio sockets (f21) — though madsim/turmoil could still cover whatever *internal* tokio networking the controller uses. The fitting approaches for Dorc's own seam are: (a) **provider-trait** — the orchestrator is generic over a `Transport`/`Executor` trait (prod = ssh-spawn+wrap per `plans/077`; sim = synthetic outcomes), monomorphized, zero prod cost; or (b) **state-machine + message-bus** (sled/Polar Signals) — plan/apply as state machines, "run leaf L on host H" becomes a *message*, fault injection lives only in the bus, which **sidesteps the transitive-dep wall (f23)** because the kernel has no IO deps to police. [A-sled-simulation-guide-2020][B-polarsignals-dst-state-machines-2025]
- f29. **`kFIDELITY` gains a third mode.** `plans/077` has optimized-vs-faithful; DST adds a third: **optimized** (prod, batched/hoisted) / **faithful** (un-optimized, real-exec, attributable 1:1) / **simulated** (in-process, synthetic outcomes, no real exec). The faithful mode's "one leaf, one execution, control-flow preserved" discipline is *the same* 1:1 leaf↔decision mapping a DST sim needs — so `plans/077`'s anti-batching requirement and the DST seam want the same structural property. +SURE (dual-use again).
- f30. **The transitive-dep wall (f23) is *smaller* for Dorc than for a DB — by construction.** Dorc's analyzer kernel is near-pure (AST→facts); the orchestrator's nondeterminism is concentrated at the `dorc_exec` seam, which Dorc controls by design (the whole point of `plans/077`). The most dangerous "dependency" — the remote host — is not a crate to `[patch]` or a libc call to override; it is THE seam you mock. So Dorc's "deterministic kernel" can be large and its "nondeterministic edges" few and explicit, *if* the orchestrator avoids reaching for wall-clock/rand/threads directly (the state-machine or provider-trait disciplines enforce this). +SURE→~SUSPECT.

## The `axis-dst-cost` ladder (laid out for the deferred decision — NOT decided here)
- **L0 · Reserve-the-seam (cheap; recommended to *bank* day-1):** make `dorc_exec` a clean trait/message boundary (already required by `plans/077` for tracing) and keep orchestrator logic free of direct clock/rand/IO (inject them). Cost ≈ nil beyond `plans/077`. Buys: DST stays *possible*; this is the only retrofit-hostile piece. Aligns with priorities #1/#2 (no maintainability tax).
- **L1 · In-process orchestrator sim (medium):** a synthetic-outcome backend + a seeded scheduler that fuzzes drop/timeout/partition/reorder/crash across the multi-host DAG, asserts invariants after every step, prints+replays the seed (sled-recipe-sized; [A-sled-simulation-guide-2020]). Tests Dorc's *differentiating* correctness (f25-CAN). Cost: a real but bounded harness; no transitive-dep grind if the kernel is state-machine/provider-trait.
- **L2 · Full DST (expensive; likely overkill):** control *all* nondeterminism incl. internal tokio/threads/transitive deps (the f23 grind, libc overrides, single-thread+async everywhere). Heavy maintainability/simplicity tax (`axis-dst-cost` vs #1/#2), and the marginal gain is small because Dorc's edge is mocked anyway (f26).
- **L3 · Buy Antithesis (later; defense-in-depth):** containerize Dorc + the fakes, run in the hypervisor. Cheap-to-add-later, retrofit-friendly (the f2 demotion) — explicitly *not* a day-1 concern.

**Read:** L0 is the day-1 bank (cheap, retrofit-hostile, dual-use with `plans/077`). L1 is the plausible sweet spot *if* f26 resolves toward "the orchestrator carries real complexity." L2/L3 are deferred/likely-skip. This is the shape of the `axis-dst-cost` decision; the decision itself waits (human dictate).

## Citations
> [A-fdb-sigmod-paper-2021]:§3 Simulation Testing / Limitations (relevance: +1:SURE) — the in-process deterministic sim, and the edge it cannot test
> the real database software is run, together with randomized synthetic workloads and fault injection, in a deterministic discrete-event simulation… All database code is deterministic; accordingly multithreaded concurrency is avoided (instead, one database node is deployed per core).
> The production implementation is a simple shim to the relevant system calls.
> Limitations. Simulation … is also unable to test third-party libraries or dependencies, or even first-party code not implemented in Flow. As a consequence, we have largely avoided taking dependencies on external systems… bugs in critical dependent systems, such as a filesystem or the operating system, or misunderstandings of their contract, can lead to bugs in FDB.

> [A-sled-simulation-guide-2020]:§recipe (relevance: +1:SURE) — the orchestrator-as-state-machine fit, run on a sim in test and real transport in prod
> write your algorithm around a state machine that receives messages from other nodes, and responds with the set of outgoing messages. this can be easily run on the simulator in testing, and on top of a real tcp/whatever transport in production.

> [B-polarsignals-dst-state-machines-2025]:§architecture (relevance: +1:SURE) — deps-as-messages centralizes fault injection and sidesteps the transitive-dep wall
> this failure injection needs to be implemented only once in the message bus to apply to all state machines (including all dependencies).

## Human verdict + open questions (round 12, after Front A; recorded in AGENTS)
- **Lean (provisional; defer the rung):** heavily pro **in-process-DST-discipline** now — bank L0 (reserve the seam) and write the kernel in the async / inverted-control style that keeps DST cheap — worry about climbing to L1+ later. Not married to it.
- **Why DST is unusually cheap *here* vs the literature:** (1) Rust's swap story is zero-prod-cost (cfg-swap / provider-trait / state-machine — the human's macros instinct); (2) the async + single-thread + inverted-control discipline the literature counts as DST's main cost is ~free for an async-native dev, and IoC is independently warranted for several Dorc components (cf. Menhir's parser error-handling architecture). Residual cost = (A) one upfront seam choice + (B) crate-gating — and **(B) bites only deps reachable from the simulated kernel**, never prod/edge-only deps (a terminal-colour lib needs no mock-friendliness). Keeping the important bits dependency-light shrinks (B) further.
- **Open Q1 — transport undecided (updates f21/f28):** "SSH a script over" is a *starting point*, not a design goal; an *agentless-but-temporary-executor* model (ship a single-binary Dorc executor that phones home as it runs) is live. If it wins, the controller↔executor channel **is** a tokio-socket-shaped transport → madsim/turmoil become directly applicable; do not foreclose them.
- **Open Q2 — orchestrator ownership undecided (updates f25/f26):** whether Dorc owns its orchestrator at all is open (could build on pyinfra or similar). If coordination is delegated, the DST-testable kernel shrinks and the delegate becomes a mocked edge — re-evaluate f26 then.
